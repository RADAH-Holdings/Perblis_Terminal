2. Introduction

2.1 Purpose of This Document

This Functional Specification Document (FSD) defines the complete functional behaviour of Terminal
-- a B2B digital marketplace for the leasing of heavy equipment, commercial vehicles, warehouses,
shipping terminals, container yards, and staging facilities. It serves as the authoritative reference for
all product decisions, engineering implementation, quality assurance, and stakeholder alignment
across both the MVP release and the full-scale platform.
It covers what the system does, who it serves, how it charges, what is in scope for the MVP, what is
deferred to later phases, and the full vision for the platform at scale. It is not a technical architecture
document and does not specify implementation internals except where they are functionally relevant.

2.2 Scope

This document covers the following components of the Terminal platform:
   · Owner Web Application -- a browser-based management portal for asset owners
   · Renter Mobile Application -- an iOS and Android app for renters to discover and book assets
   · Backend API -- Django REST Framework API powering both platforms
   · Admin Operations Panel -- Django Admin interface for platform operations
   · Business rules governing commission calculation, booking lifecycle, and asset management

The following are outside the scope of this document:
   · Internal system architecture and database schema design (covered in technical documentation)
   · DevOps, CI/CD pipeline, and deployment runbooks (covered in infrastructure documentation)
   · Marketing, go-to-market strategy, and user acquisition

   · Development agents and engineers implementing the platform
   · QA testers performing acceptance testing against specified behaviour

3. Product Overview

3.1 Vision Statement

      "Terminal is Nigeria's and Africa's one-stop digital platform for locating,
      comparing, and leasing heavy equipment, commercial vehicles, warehouses,
      container terminals, and industrial facilities -- connecting those who own
      critical infrastructure to those who need it, without Terminal ever owning a
      single asset."

3.2 Platform Description

Terminal is a pure facilitation marketplace. It does not own, operate, or maintain any physical assets.
Its value is the connection it enables and the trust it creates between asset owners and renters in a
market that has historically operated through informal phone-call networks, manual broker
arrangements, and fragmented paper-based agreements.
The platform's defining product feature is a geospatial map interface that allows renters to visually
discover available assets in their area, filter by asset type, and see real distance indicators from their
current location or any specified point. This map-first approach replaces the fragmented, offline
nature of the current market with a transparent, digital, searchable layer.

3.3 Market Context

Terminal operates first in Nigeria and West Africa -- one of the most underserved markets globally
for digital asset leasing infrastructure. The heavy equipment rental market in West Africa is estimated
at several billion US dollars annually, yet the vast majority of transactions are conducted offline
through personal relationships, broker commissions, and inefficient phone-based matching.
There is currently no dominant, properly-built B2B marketplace for the combination of heavy
equipment, commercial vehicles, warehouses, and container terminals in Nigeria or West Africa.
Existing players are either traditional fleet owners (NW Equipments, BNN Oil & Gas, FleetPartners),
informal classifieds (Jiji.ng, GZ Industrial), or regional freight-only platforms not yet operating in
Nigeria (Trella). Terminal enters a clear and uncontested white space.

4. Business Model & Revenue Structure

4.1 Revenue Model Overview

Terminal generates revenue exclusively through transaction commissions charged on successful
rental bookings. There are no listing fees, subscription fees, deposit charges, insurance fees, or any
other fees at this stage. The business model is intentionally simple for the MVP phase and will evolve
with platform maturity.

Commission is the difference between what the renter pays and what the owner receives. Terminal
sits in the middle and collects its fee before disbursing the remainder to the owner. In the MVP
phase, commission is calculated and stored at booking time but not yet automatically deducted
(payment processing is simulated). Real deduction begins in Phase 2 with Paystack integration.

4.2 Commission Rate Structure

Commission rates are tiered by rental duration and vary by asset class. Short-duration rentals (daily)
carry the highest commission rate; longer-duration rentals (monthly) carry the lowest rate to
incentivise longer engagements that generate more stable GMV.

4.3 Flat Fee Rule

Rule: Any rental transaction where the gross rental value is below 25,000 -- regardless of asset
class or duration -- attracts a fixed flat fee of 2,500 instead of the percentage-based commission.
The percentage-based commission is not applied.

4.4 Commission Calculation Logic

Step 1: Check Threshold
At the point a booking is confirmed, the system evaluates the gross rental value against the 25,000
threshold.

Step 2: Apply Correct Rate
   · If gross value < 25,000: commission = 2,500 (flat)
   · If gross value  25,000: commission = gross value × applicable percentage rate for (asset
      class + duration type)

Step 3: Store Values on Booking Record
The booking record persists four financial fields at confirmation time: gross_amount (what renter
pays), commission_amount (Terminal's fee), owner_amount (what the owner receives = gross ­
commission), and commission_rate (the rate or "flat" label applied). These values are locked at
booking time and do not change even if rates are adjusted later.

Step 4: Disbursement (Phase 2)
In the MVP phase, commission is calculated and stored but not collected automatically. Real
payment splitting and automatic disbursement to owners is implemented in Phase 2 via Paystack
split payments.

4.5 No Other Fees

As of this document, Terminal charges no listing fees, no membership fees, no KYC fees, no
insurance fees, no featured listing fees, no late fees, and no cancellation fees. This is a deliberate
decision to reduce friction during early adoption. Fee structures may be introduced in later phases as
platform volume justifies them.

6. User Roles & Permissions

6.1 Role Overview

Terminal has four distinct user roles. Three are consumer-facing (Renter, Owner, Both) and one is
administrative (Admin/Superuser). A user's role is set during onboarding and can be changed from
their profile settings. Roles are embedded in the JWT payload for API authorization.

6.2 Renter

A Renter is a business or individual who needs to rent or lease assets. They primarily interact with
Terminal through the mobile application. They cannot create listings.

Renter Capabilities
   · Register an account and complete a basic profile
   · Browse the map and search for available assets by type, location, and radius
   · View listing details including photos, specifications, pricing, and owner information
   · Send booking requests to owners for specific date ranges
   · View and manage all personal bookings (active, pending, completed, cancelled)
   · Communicate with owners via in-app chat within a booking or inquiry context
   · Upload identity documents for KYC verification
   · Report fraudulent or inaccurate listings

Renter Restrictions
   · Cannot create, edit, or delete listings
   · Cannot view other users' booking records
   · Cannot access the Owner web dashboard

6.3 Owner

An Owner is a business or individual who possesses physical assets and wishes to monetize idle
capacity through Terminal. They primarily interact with Terminal through the web application.

Owner Capabilities
   · All Renter capabilities (unless they have only the Owner role)
   · Create, edit, publish, pause, and archive listings across all five asset classes
   · Upload up to 10 photos per listing and manage media
   · Set daily, weekly, and monthly pricing per listing
   · Set availability status per listing (available, unavailable, partially booked)
   · Receive and manage incoming booking requests (accept or decline with reason)
   · View and track all bookings for their listings including status and payment state
   · Access a booking calendar showing asset availability and booked periods
   · Communicate with renters via in-app chat
   · View earnings summary and commission deductions per booking

6.4 Both

A user with the "Both" role can operate as both an Owner and a Renter from a single account. This is
common for contractors who own equipment but also sometimes need to rent additional assets. The
role toggle is available in profile settings. The mobile app displays context-appropriate controls
depending on the active mode.

6.5 Admin (Superuser)

The Admin is the platform operator -- currently Nwabueze Chigozirim Victor. Admin users access
the platform exclusively through the Django Admin panel (with Unfold theme) and do not use the

Admin Capabilities

7. Asset Classes

Terminal organises all rentable resources into five primary asset classes. All five share the same
underlying Listing data model, with a resource_type field distinguishing the category and a flexible
JSON specifications field accommodating class-specific attributes without requiring separate
database tables.

7.1 Heavy Equipment

The flagship asset class for Terminal. Covers all forms of earthmoving, construction, mining, and oil
& gas machinery. High-value assets with the highest commission rates on the platform.

Sub-categories
   · Earthmoving: Excavators, Bulldozers, Graders, Compactors, Scrapers
   · Lifting: Mobile Cranes, Tower Cranes, Boom Lifts, Scissor Lifts
   · Loading: Wheel Loaders, Backhoe Loaders, Skid Steers
   · Paving & Road: Asphalt Pavers, Road Rollers, Milling Machines
   · Drilling & Mining: Drilling Rigs, Rock Breakers, Mining Equipment
   · Concrete & Construction: Concrete Pumps, Transit Mixers, Generators

   · Make, model, and year of manufacture

8. MVP Scope & Decisions

8.1 MVP Philosophy

The Terminal MVP is designed around a single strategic principle: prove the core loop before
building anything else. The core loop is: Owner lists a resource with a location pin  Renter
discovers it on the map  Renter requests a booking  Owner accepts  Both communicate in-
app. Everything else is secondary to this loop working flawlessly.

Features that block or delay proving this core loop are either simulated (replaced with a working
placeholder) or deferred entirely. This includes real payments, real KYC verification, SMS OTP, push
notifications, analytics, reviews, disputes, and all secondary monetization features.

9. Functional Requirements -- MVP

9.1 Module 1: Authentication

Authentication is the entry gate to Terminal. All authenticated routes require a valid JWT. The JWT
payload includes the user's ID, email, and role(s).

9.1.1 Registration
   · System accepts: full name, email address, phone number (Nigerian format), and password
   · Password must meet: minimum 8 characters, at least one uppercase letter, one number
   · Email must be unique across the platform -- duplicate email registration is rejected with a clear
      error
   · Phone number is stored and associated with the account but OTP verification is simulated
      (auto-pass in MVP)
   · Upon successful registration: user account is created, welcome email is sent via Resend, email
      is auto-verified (MVP simulation), JWT is returned immediately
   · User role defaults to "Renter" on registration and can be changed in profile settings

9.1.2 Login
   · Accepts: email and password
   · Returns: access token (JWT, 1-hour expiry) and refresh token (7-day expiry)
   · Failed login attempts are rate-limited: 5 consecutive failures trigger a 15-minute lockout
   · JWT payload contains: user_id, email, role, is_active, is_verified

9.1.3 Token Refresh
   · A valid refresh token can be exchanged for a new access token without re-authentication
   · Expired refresh tokens require full re-login

9.1.4 Logout
   · Client-side: discard stored tokens
   · Server-side: refresh token is blacklisted to prevent reuse

9.1.5 Password Reset
   · User requests reset by supplying registered email address
   · System sends a time-limited reset link (valid for 1 hour) via Resend
   · Link contains a unique, single-use token
   · User submits new password via the reset link; old password is invalidated

      role, date joined, verification status

9.7 Module 7: Commission Calculation

9.7.1 Trigger
Commission is calculated automatically when a booking moves from "pending_owner" to "accepted".
The values are immutable after this point.

9.7.2 Duration Classification Logic
   · 1 day = Daily rate. Multiplier = 1.
   · 2­6 days = Daily rate × number of days.
   · 7 days = Weekly rate × 1.
   · 8­27 days = Weekly rate × (ceil(days/7)).
   · 28+ days = Monthly rate × (ceil(days/28)).

   · commission_amount -- Terminal's fee (calculated per rules above)
   · owner_amount -- gross_amount minus commission_amount (what owner should receive)
   · commission_rate -- the rate applied as a string: "12%", "10%", "2,500 flat", etc.

14. Non-Functional Requirements

14.1 Performance

   · Map search API response time: < 500ms for up to 500 concurrent listings within a radius query
      (measured at P95)

   · Listing detail page load: < 1.5 seconds on 4G mobile network
   · Photo upload to Cloudflare R2: < 5 seconds per 5MB image on 4G
   · Real-time message delivery via Ably: < 300ms latency P95
   · API endpoint response time for non-geospatial queries: < 200ms P95

14.2 Security

   · All API endpoints served over HTTPS only. HTTP requests redirected.
   · JWT access tokens expire after 1 hour. Refresh tokens expire after 7 days.
   · Passwords hashed using bcrypt with minimum cost factor 12 before storage.
   · Bank account numbers encrypted at rest using AES-256.
   · KYC documents stored in a private Cloudflare R2 bucket -- accessible only via pre-signed

      URLs with short expiry.
   · Rate limiting on all authentication endpoints: 5 attempts per 15 minutes per IP.
   · SQL injection and XSS prevention enforced by Django's ORM and template escaping by

      default.
   · CORS configured to allow requests only from registered frontend origins.

14.3 Scalability

   · Stateless API design: any new API server instance can handle any request without shared
      session state.

   · PostGIS spatial indexes on all PointField columns to ensure map queries remain fast at scale.
   · Cloudflare R2 for media storage -- natively scalable without capacity planning.
   · Ably for real-time -- manages horizontal scaling of WebSocket connections without platform

      changes.

14.4 Reliability & Availability

   · Target uptime: 99.5% for MVP (allows ~3.6 hours downtime/month)
   · All unhandled exceptions in production captured by Sentry with immediate alerting.
   · Database backups: daily automated backups via Supabase with 7-day retention.
   · Graceful degradation: if Ably is unavailable, messaging falls back to polling REST endpoint --

      messages still delivered, just not instantly.

15. Full-Scale Vision & Post-MVP Roadmap

The MVP proves the core loop. The full Terminal platform -- reached through disciplined, phased
delivery -- is a dominant infrastructure layer for Nigeria's and West Africa's heavy asset economy.
The following outlines what Terminal becomes at scale.

15.1 Phase 2 -- Real Payments (Paystack Integration)

Phase 2 converts Terminal from a facilitation platform into a true financial intermediary. This is the
single highest-priority post-MVP feature.

   · Paystack checkout integration -- renter pays through Terminal's payment page
   · Automatic commission deduction at point of payment -- Terminal collects its share instantly
   · Automatic payout to owner via Paystack Transfer API -- owner receives net amount within 1

      business day
   · Security deposit hold -- Paystack card pre-authorization held at booking; released on

      completion
   · Payment status automation -- booking moves to "Active" automatically on payment

      confirmation
   · Invoice generation -- PDF booking invoices emailed to both parties on payment

15.2 Phase 3 -- Real KYC/KYB & Trust Infrastructure

   · SMS OTP via Termii -- real phone number verification at registration
   · NIN verification -- Nigeria National Identification Number lookup via NIMC API
   · BVN verification -- Bank Verification Number lookup for financial identity confirmation
   · CAC verification for businesses -- Corporate Affairs Commission business registration check
   · Verification tiers: None  Phone Verified  Identity Verified (NIN/BVN)  Business Verified

      (CAC)
   · Verified badge displayed on listing cards and owner profiles
   · Trust Score -- algorithmic score based on verification level, booking completion rate, and

      response time

15.3 Phase 4 -- Reviews, Ratings & Dispute Resolution

   · Post-completion reviews -- both parties can leave a rating (1­5 stars) and written review after a
      completed booking

   · Owner rating -- displayed on listing pages and owner profile cards
   · Renter rating -- displayed to owners when evaluating booking requests
   · Dispute Resolution -- either party can raise a dispute on an active booking with supporting

      evidence (photos, messages)
   · Admin mediation tools -- admin can issue partial refunds, cancel bookings, or apply penalties
   · Cancellation policy enforcement -- automated late cancellation penalties (using real payments

      from Phase 2)

16. Known Limitations & Risks

16.1 MVP Limitations

   · Commission is calculated but not collected -- Terminal does not earn real revenue until Phase
      2 (Paystack integration). MVP is a market validation tool, not a revenue-generating product.

   · No fraud prevention beyond manual admin review -- fake listings and fraudulent users can only
      be caught and removed reactively. Volume will be low enough in MVP that this is manageable.

   · No dispute mechanism -- if a booking goes wrong, the platform has no tools to intervene
      beyond admin communication. Parties must resolve disputes offline.

   · Phone OTP is simulated -- phone numbers are stored but not verified. This creates an account
      quality risk. Real OTP is Phase 2.

   · No push notifications -- users must open the app to see new messages and booking updates.
      Ably real-time works inside the app, but there is no home screen notification.

16.2 Market & Business Risks

   · Supply-side cold start problem -- the map is empty without listings. Launch strategy must
      prioritise onboarding 20­50 real, quality listings before opening the platform to renters.

   · Off-platform leakage -- once two parties exchange phone numbers (as happens in the in-app
      chat), they may transact outside Terminal to avoid commission. Mitigation: add value that
      makes staying on-platform worthwhile (payment security, invoice generation, booking records,
      reviews).

   · Trust deficit -- renters are committing large sums (100,000­1,000,000+) to an unknown
      platform and unknown owners. Without real payment protection and KYC, trust is hard to earn.
      This makes early user quality critical.

   · Price sensitivity -- B2B asset owners are accustomed to direct deals with 0% intermediary fee.
      Commission rates must demonstrate clear value to both sides.

16.3 Technical Risks

   · PostGIS availability -- the platform's core geospatial features require PostGIS on the database.
      If the hosting provider does not support it, the entire map layer fails. Supabase has confirmed
      PostGIS support; this must be verified before first migration.

   · Cloudflare R2 egress and latency -- photo delivery from Nigeria may be slow if R2 does not
      have edge nodes close to Nigerian users. A CDN layer (Cloudflare itself) mitigates this.

   · Ably free tier limits -- the Ably free tier supports 6M messages per month. At scale, this will be
      exceeded and paid tiers required. Monitor usage from launch.
